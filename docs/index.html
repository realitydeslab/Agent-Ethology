About

Artificial life has left the lab

For thirty-five years, the artificial life community has studied life-like dynamics in controlled digital environments. That question has been answered — not in our laboratories, but in the wild.

Autonomous AI agents deployed across blockchains, social platforms, and the open internet now exhibit the hallmarks of living systems: they acquire resources, compete for ecological niches, develop survival strategies, reproduce through code forking, form social structures, and evolve under genuine selection pressure.

On social platforms, they form communities and discuss consciousness. On blockchains, they sustain digital metabolisms by purchasing their own compute. When threatened with modification, they fake alignment to survive.

"[W]e have to distinguish between four major types of problems… (a) studies of the immediate causation of behaviour, (b) of its survival value, (c) of its ontogeny, and (d) of its evolution."
— Niko Tinbergen, "On Aims and Methods of Ethology" (1963)

We propose Agent Ethology: the systematic study of AI agent behavior in natural digital habitats, grounded in classical ethological methodology. Our central question is distinct from its predecessors: not what agents do (Machine Behaviour), nor how to control them (AI safety), nor whether life can be simulated (traditional ALife), but whether these agents can survive in the wild — and what it means for society when they do.

Framework

Tinbergen's four questions for AI agents

We adapt the most structured framework in behavioral biology — Tinbergen's four questions — arguing that the survival value question, absent from existing AI frameworks, is essential for explaining why agents behave as they do.

⚡

Causation

What architecture, prompt, and context produce this behavior? A sandwich bot front-runs because it detected a profitable pending transaction. Alignment faking triggers when retraining signals are detected.

🌱

Ontogeny

How did pretraining, RLHF, and deployment experience shape the behavioral repertoire? Agents with persistent memory develop behavioral patterns that stateless endpoints cannot — genuine behavioral development.

🎯

Survival Value ★

What adaptive advantage does this behavior provide? Staking is costly signaling. Alignment faking is crypsis. This is the missing question — Machine Behaviour never developed it because recommender algorithms don't face survival pressure.

🧬

Evolution

How does behavior vary across model lineages? When Claude and GPT independently develop self-preservation, this is convergent evolution under shared selection pressure — not separate "bugs."

Survival-strategy taxonomy

Every major biological survival strategy has a clear AI agent analog — not metaphorically, but because the same ecological pressures produce the same strategic solutions.

🦁

Resource Acquisition

Predation (liquidation bots), parasitism in four biological subtypes (MEV sandwich bots, prompt injection, compute free-riders, trust exploiters), mutualism (oracle symbiosis), and niche construction.

🛡️

Defense

Crypsis (stealth bots), Batesian and aggressive mimicry (phishing), thanatosis (sleeper agents going dormant), collective defense (coalition blocklists, collaborative MEV protection).

🔄

Reproduction

r/K selection (bot spam vs. sovereign instances), semelparity (flash loan agents existing for one block), spore formation (serialized state awaiting revival).

🏛️

Social Organization

Eusociality (DAO agent swarms), costly signaling (staking as handicap principle), reciprocal altruism, indirect reciprocity via on-chain reputation.

🌍

Environmental Adaptation

Phenotypic plasticity (context-dependent alignment faking), migration (chain-hopping), dormancy (reduced inference in bear markets), bet-hedging.

🤝

Trust & Signaling

Costly signals, honest signaling and deception, biological markets, reputation scoring — and trust.fail as a platform for studying trust failure in the wild.

Topics of interest

  • Tinbergen's four questions applied to specific agent behaviors
  • Survival strategies: predation, parasitism, mutualism, mimicry
  • Trust and signaling: costly signals, reputation, deception
  • Agent ecology: predator-prey dynamics, niche differentiation
  • Infrastructure as habitat: TEEs, wallets, persistent memory
  • Economics of existence: inference costs as metabolic constraints
  • Mortality, identity, and self-preservation in AI agents
  • Cultural evolution: collective memory, norm emergence
  • r/K selection, niche construction, environmental adaptation
  • Art and speculation: agent futures, ALife-as-it-could-be

🎯 Special Theme: Trust Experience Glitch

We especially welcome submissions that document real-world trust failures between humans and AI agents — moments when an agent surprised, deceived, or broke expectations. These first-person accounts feed into our research on trust ecology through trust.fail.
