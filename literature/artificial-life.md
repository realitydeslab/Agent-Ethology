# Artificial Life — Literature Review

1. **Langton, C.G. (1989). "Artificial Life." *ALife Proceedings*, 1–47. Addison-Wesley.**
   - Coined "Artificial Life"; life-as-it-could-be.
   - Relevance: Foundational framing for studying artificial agent life.

2. **Langton, C.G. (1990). "Computation at the Edge of Chaos." *Physica D*, 42(1-3), 12–37.**
   - Edge of chaos as optimal for computation and emergence.
   - Relevance: Conditions for emergent agent behaviors.

3. **Bedau, M.A. (2003). "Artificial Life: Organization, Adaptation, Complexity." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(11), 505–512.**
   - ALife as bottom-up study of life-like phenomena.
   - Relevance: Bottom-up emergence framework for agent ethology.

4. **Ray, T.S. (1991). "An Approach to the Synthesis of Life." *ALife II*, 371–408.**
   - Tierra: self-replicating digital organisms with parasitism and ecology.
   - Relevance: Seminal digital ecosystem with parasitism, mortality, ecology.

5. **Ofria, C. & Wilke, C.O. (2004). "Avida." *Artificial Life*, 10(2), 191–229.**
   - Platform for studying evolution of digital organisms.
   - Relevance: Major platform for agent evolution and behavioral ecology.

6. **Lenski, R.E. et al. (2003). "The Evolutionary Origin of Complex Features." *Nature*, 423, 139–144.**
   - Evolution of complex functions in Avida.
   - Relevance: Emergence of complex agent behaviors.

7. **Adami, C. (1998). *Introduction to Artificial Life*. Springer.**
   - Textbook on ALife including digital evolution and self-replication.
   - Relevance: Comprehensive ALife foundations.

8. **Kauffman, S.A. (1993). *The Origins of Order*. Oxford UP.**
   - Self-organization as complement to selection; order for free.
   - Relevance: Self-organization for understanding emergent agent behaviors.

9. **Camazine, S. et al. (2001). *Self-Organization in Biological Systems*. Princeton UP.**
   - Self-organization in biology.
   - Relevance: Models applicable to agent ecosystem dynamics.

10. **Wolfram, S. (2002). *A New Kind of Science*. Wolfram Media.**
    - Cellular automata as universal substrates; emergence from simple rules.
    - Relevance: Emergence framework for agent behaviors.

11. **Conway, J. (1970). "The Game of Life." *Scientific American* (Gardner's column).**
    - Emergent patterns from simple rules.
    - Relevance: Canonical emergence example.

12. **Stanley, K.O. & Lehman, J. (2015). *Why Greatness Cannot Be Planned*. Springer.**
    - Open-ended search without fixed objectives produces novelty.
    - Relevance: Open-ended agent evolution.

13. **Taylor, T. et al. (2016). "Open-Ended Evolution." *Artificial Life*, 22(3), 408–423.**
    - Requirements for open-ended evolution.
    - Relevance: Conditions for continually novel agent behaviors.

14. **Packard, N. et al. (2019). "An Overview of Open-Ended Evolution." *Artificial Life*, 25(2), 93–103.**
    - State-of-the-art review on OEE.
    - Relevance: Framework for ever-diversifying agent repertoires.

15. **Maturana, H.R. & Varela, F.J. (1980). *Autopoiesis and Cognition*. D. Reidel.**
    - Self-producing systems as definition of life.
    - Relevance: Framework for sovereign self-maintaining agents.

16. **Varela, F.J. et al. (1991). *The Embodied Mind*. MIT Press.**
    - Enactive cognition; cognition as embodied action.
    - Relevance: Agent cognition as situated behavioral ecology.

17. **McMullin, B. (2004). "Thirty Years of Computational Autopoiesis." *Artificial Life*, 10(3), 277–295.**
    - Review of computational autopoiesis implementations.
    - Relevance: Computational autopoiesis for self-sustaining agents.

18. **Holland, J.H. (1975). *Adaptation in Natural and Artificial Systems*. U Michigan Press.**
    - Genetic algorithms; adaptation through evolutionary computation.
    - Relevance: Evolutionary framework for agent adaptation.

19. **Koza, J.R. (1992). *Genetic Programming*. MIT Press.**
    - Evolving programs through genetic programming.
    - Relevance: Evolution of agent behavioral programs.

20. **Smith, J.M. & Szathmáry, E. (1995). *The Major Transitions in Evolution*. Oxford UP.**
    - Major evolutionary transitions: molecules → cells → multicellularity → societies.
    - Relevance: Framework for transitions in agent complexity.

21. **Nowak, M.A. (2006). *Evolutionary Dynamics*. Harvard UP.**
    - Mathematical framework for evolutionary dynamics.
    - Relevance: Models for agent population dynamics.

22. **Fontana, W. & Buss, L.W. (1994). "The Arrival of the Fittest." *Bull. Math. Biol.*, 56(1), 1–64.**
    - AlChemy model of molecular self-organization.
    - Relevance: Theoretical framework for emergent agent organization.
