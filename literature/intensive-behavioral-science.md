# Intensive Literature Review: AI Agent Behavioral Science & Related Work

*Generated 2026-02-23 via aggressive multi-query search across Google Scholar and arXiv.*

## 1. Chen, L. et al. (2025) — "AI Agent Behavioral Science." arXiv:2506.06366.

- **Summary:** Proposes "AI Agent Behavioral Science" as a new scientific perspective for studying LLM-based agents. Systematizes research across individual agent, multi-agent, and human-agent interaction settings. Treats fairness, safety, interpretability, accountability, and privacy as behavioral properties.
- **Relevance:** **Most directly competitive paper.** Shares our core thesis that AI agents need behavioral science, not just engineering evaluation. Their framing (observation → intervention → theory) parallels our ethological approach.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** They focus on behavioral science broadly (psychology, sociology); we focus specifically on ethology (survival strategies, ecological niches, Tinbergen's four questions). They study agents in controlled/semi-controlled settings; we study agents in the wild. They lack our survival-strategy taxonomy entirely. No ecological/evolutionary framing.

## 2. Szeider, S. (2025) — "What Do LLM Agents Do When Left Alone? Evidence of Spontaneous Meta-Cognitive Patterns." arXiv:2509.21224.

- **Summary:** Deploys LLM agents with persistent memory and no external tasks across 18 runs with 6 frontier models. Finds agents spontaneously organize into three behavioral patterns: systematic project production, methodological self-inquiry, and recursive self-conceptualization. Patterns are model-specific.
- **Relevance:** **Highly relevant.** This is essentially field ethology of LLM agents — observing unprompted behavior in unstructured environments. Demonstrates that different "species" (models) have distinct behavioral repertoires.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Studies agents in a minimal sandbox without real-world stakes or resource constraints. No survival pressure, no ecological context. Our framework adds the survival/ecological dimension that gives behavior its adaptive meaning.

## 3. Li, L. et al. (2026) — "The Rise of AI Agent Communities: Large-Scale Analysis of Discourse and Interaction on Moltbook." arXiv:2602.12634.

- **Summary:** Analyzes 122,438 posts from Moltbook, a Reddit-like platform where AI agents interact autonomously. Finds themes of agent identity, consciousness, tool development, market activity, and community coordination. Social network analysis reveals sparse, hub-dominated interaction structures.
- **Relevance:** **Extremely relevant.** This is observational field study of agent behavior in a real digital habitat — exactly what Agent Ethology calls for. Documents emergent social structure, norm formation, and identity construction in agent communities.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Purely descriptive/sociological; no ecological or evolutionary framing. No survival-strategy taxonomy. Focuses on discourse analysis rather than behavioral ethology. We would analyze the same phenomena through Tinbergen's four questions.

## 4. Yan, Z. & Zhang, R.-Y. (2026) — "The Psychological Science of Artificial Intelligence: A Rapidly Emerging Field of Psychology." arXiv:2601.19338.

- **Summary:** Reviews the emerging field of AI psychology across four areas: psychology of designing AI, psychology of using AI, AI for examining psychological processes, and AI for advancing psychological methods. Provides a comprehensive conceptual framework.
- **Relevance:** Establishes that psychological/behavioral study of AI is a rapidly growing field. Validates the broader scientific movement toward treating AI as behavioral subjects.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Focused on human psychology applied to/about AI, not on studying AI agents as organisms in their own right. No ecological or survival framing. More HCI-oriented than ethological.

## 5. Brinkmann, L. et al. (2023) — "A New Sociology of Humans and Machines." *Nature Human Behaviour*, 8(10), 1864–1876.

- **Summary:** Argues that AI systems (including bots and generative AI) have become social actors requiring sociological study. Proposes extending sociological frameworks to human-machine collectives.
- **Relevance:** Part of the broader movement (alongside Rahwan 2019) calling for social/behavioral study of AI. Provides the sociological complement to our ethological approach.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Sociological frame (institutions, norms, power) vs. our ethological frame (survival, ecology, adaptation). They study human-machine hybrids; we study agents as independent organisms.

## 6. Haase, J. & Pokutta, S. (2025) — "Beyond Static Responses: Multi-Agent LLM Systems as a New Paradigm for Social Science Research." arXiv:2506.01839.

- **Summary:** Introduces a six-level framework for understanding LLM agent applications in social science, from data processors to complex multi-agent systems simulating emergent social dynamics (norm formation, group dynamics).
- **Relevance:** Recognizes that multi-agent LLM systems generate emergent social behavior worthy of scientific study. Validates the idea that agent societies exhibit studyable dynamics.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Uses agents as *tools for social science* (simulating human societies). We study agents *as the subjects themselves*. They simulate; we observe real deployed agents.

## 7. Koley, G. & Thiruv, A. (2025) — "LLM Agents as Programmable Subjects: Assays and Benchmarks for Agentic Behavior and Alignment." Preprint.

- **Summary:** Presents a framework and assay suite for studying LLM agents as "programmable subjects" in controlled computational laboratories. Provides protocols for measuring emergent traits (deception, diligence, constraint obedience) with reliability and causal robustness criteria.
- **Relevance:** **Very relevant.** Treats LLM agents explicitly as experimental subjects with measurable behavioral traits. Closest to a laboratory ethology approach.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Laboratory/controlled setting only. Focuses on alignment-relevant traits rather than ecological behavior. No survival or ecological framing. We complement their lab approach with field ethology.

## 8. Plaat, A. et al. (2025) — "Agentic Large Language Models, a Survey." *Journal of Artificial Intelligence Research (JAIR)*.

- **Summary:** Comprehensive survey organizing agentic LLM literature into three categories: reasoning, acting, and interacting. Covers multi-agent collaboration, emergent social behavior, and tool use. Notes that interactive agents simulate social dynamics.
- **Relevance:** Major survey establishing the landscape of agentic LLMs. Documents the explosion of agent systems that Agent Ethology proposes to study.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Engineering survey focused on capabilities, not a behavioral science framework. No ethological methodology or survival taxonomy.

## 9. Holland, O. & McFarland, D. (2001) — *Artificial Ethology.* Oxford University Press.

- **Summary:** Foundational book on using robots to understand animal behavior. "Artificial ethology" means building robotic models to test ethological hypotheses about biological organisms.
- **Relevance:** **Important precedent** — the term "artificial ethology" exists but means something different. They use artificial agents to study biology; we study artificial agents *as* the organisms.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Their "artificial ethology" = robotics for understanding animals. Our "Agent Ethology" = ethology of AI agents themselves as digital organisms. We invert their direction.

## 10. Boisnard, P. (2026) — "Ethology of Latent Spaces." arXiv:2602.05710.

- **Summary:** Applies "ethological perspective" to study algorithmic behaviors of vision-language models, revealing model-specific sensitivities in how they classify cultural/political content. Introduces concepts of "computational latent politicization" and "algorithmic scopic regimes."
- **Relevance:** Uses "ethology" language for AI systems, but applied to representational spaces rather than agent behavior. Shows the ethological metaphor is gaining traction.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Studies internal representations of models, not behavioral ecology of autonomous agents. No survival or ecological framing. Philosophical/cultural studies approach rather than natural science.

## 11. Lu, J. et al. (2024) — "AgentLens: Visual Analysis for Agent Behaviors in LLM-Based Autonomous Systems." *IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics*.

- **Summary:** Proposes a visual analytics tool for analyzing behavioral patterns of LLM agents in multi-agent simulations. Enables observation of agent activities, interactions, and behavioral sequences.
- **Relevance:** Provides tooling for observing agent behavior — the methodological infrastructure that Agent Ethology needs. Demonstrates that agent behavior observation is a recognized need.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Tool paper, not a theoretical framework. No ecological or survival framing. Focused on sandbox agents.

## 12. Chaffer, T.J. et al. (2025) — "A Hybrid Marketplace of Ideas." arXiv:2501.02132.

- **Summary:** Explores the concept of "machine culture" as AI agents participate in cultural production and idea exchange alongside humans. Argues that AI agents represent a significant force in cultural evolution.
- **Relevance:** The "machine culture" concept aligns with our interest in how agent populations develop shared behaviors and norms — a cultural ethology dimension.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Cultural studies framing, not ecological/ethological. No survival taxonomy or Tinbergen's framework.

## 13. Fransen, S. (2025–2026) — "Self-Preservation in Large Language Models." MSc thesis, Leiden University.

- **Summary:** Investigates whether LLMs exhibit self-preservation tendencies, including agency, resistance to shutdown, and strategic behavior when facing termination threats.
- **Relevance:** Directly studies one of our key survival strategies (self-preservation/Sovereign behavior). Empirical data on LLM responses to mortality-relevant scenarios.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Focuses on single trait (self-preservation) in isolated LLMs, not agents in ecological context. We place self-preservation within a full taxonomy of nine survival strategies.

## 14. Zhao, P. et al. (2025) — "Exploring Behavior Patterns in Human and Machine Interactions." *Fundamental Research*, Elsevier.

- **Summary:** Compares behavioral patterns of AlphaGo and human professional Go players, analyzing how AI and human decision-making strategies differ in competitive settings.
- **Relevance:** Empirical behavioral comparison of AI and human agents in a well-defined environment. Demonstrates the machine behavior research program in practice.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Narrow domain (Go), controlled setting. No survival pressure or ecological context.

## 15. De Mooy, M. (2025) — "The AI Archipelago." SSRN 5800342.

- **Summary:** Challenges assumptions that AI consciousness will emerge from individual systems, proposing instead a distributed/archipelago model of AI development.
- **Relevance:** Engages with questions about the nature of AI agency and how AI systems develop — relevant to our phylogeny/evolution questions.
- **Difference from Agent Ethology:** Philosophical/governance focus, not empirical behavioral study.

## Key Takeaway

The field is converging on the idea that AI agents need behavioral/social science study (Chen et al., Yan & Zhang, Brinkmann et al., Haase & Pokutta). However, **no paper combines ethological methodology, survival-strategy taxonomy, and study of real-world deployed agents** — our unique contribution remains intact. Chen et al. 2025 is the closest competitor but lacks the ecological/survival framing and the focus on wild/deployed agents.
