# Parasitic Computing — Literature Review

## Foundational Work

1. **Barabási, A.-L., Freeh, V.W., Jeong, H., & Brockman, J.B. (2001). "Parasitic Computing." *Nature*, 412(6850), 894–897.**
   - Demonstrated computation by exploiting TCP/IP checksum of remote servers without consent.
   - Relevance: Seminal paper defining parasitic computing; foundational for parasitic agent concept.

2. **Hardin, G. (1968). "The Tragedy of the Commons." *Science*, 162(3859), 1243–1248.**
   - Classic analysis of shared resource depletion through self-interest.
   - Relevance: Framework for free-riding and resource exploitation by parasitic agents.

3. **Adar, E. & Huberman, B.A. (2000). "Free Riding on Gnutella." *First Monday*, 5(10).**
   - ~70% of Gnutella users free-ride, consuming without contributing.
   - Relevance: Empirical evidence of parasitic behavior in distributed systems.

4. **Feldman, M. et al. (2004). "Robust Incentive Techniques for P2P Networks." *EC '04*, 102–111.**
   - Mechanism design to prevent free-riding in P2P networks.
   - Relevance: Countermeasures against parasitic agent behaviors.

5. **Shneidman, J. & Parkes, D.C. (2003). "Rationality and Self-Interest in P2P Networks." *IPTPS*, 139–148.**
   - Rational self-interest leading to parasitic behavior.
   - Relevance: Game-theoretic framework for why agents become parasitic.

6. **Littman, M.L. (1994). "Markov Games as Framework for Multi-Agent RL." *ICML*, 157–163.**
   - Framework for competitive/adversarial multi-agent learning.
   - Relevance: Formal framework for adversarial (parasitic) agent interactions.

7. **Goodfellow, I.J. et al. (2015). "Explaining and Harnessing Adversarial Examples." *ICLR*.**
   - Adversarial perturbations exploiting neural network vulnerabilities.
   - Relevance: Parasitic exploitation of agent perception systems.

8. **Gleave, A. et al. (2020). "Adversarial Policies: Attacking Deep RL." *ICLR*.**
   - Adversarial policies exploiting learned behaviors of RL agents.
   - Relevance: Directly demonstrates parasitic agent strategies.

9. **Sandholm, T.W. & Crites, R.H. (1996). "Multiagent RL in the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma." *Biosystems*, 37(1-2), 147–166.**
   - RL agents learning exploitative strategies.
   - Relevance: Emergence of parasitic strategies through learning.

10. **Nowak, M.A. & May, R.M. (1992). "Evolutionary Games and Spatial Chaos." *Nature*, 359, 826–829.**
    - Cooperators and defectors coexisting in spatial games.
    - Relevance: Spatial dynamics of parasitic vs cooperative agent strategies.

11. **Axelrod, R. (1984). *The Evolution of Cooperation*. Basic Books.**
    - Tournament analysis of cooperation vs defection (Tit-for-Tat).
    - Relevance: When agents cooperate vs exploit.

12. **Nisan, N. et al. (Eds.) (2007). *Algorithmic Game Theory*. Cambridge UP.**
    - Mechanism design for selfish agents.
    - Relevance: Designing systems robust to parasitic behavior.

13. **Dawkins, R. (1982). *The Extended Phenotype*. Oxford UP.**
    - Parasites manipulating host behavior.
    - Relevance: Parasitic agents manipulating host system behaviors.

14. **Peysakhovich, A. & Lerer, A. (2018). "Prosocial Learning Agents." *AAMAS*.**
    - Prosocial agents outperform selfish ones in coordination.
    - Relevance: Counter to parasitic strategies.

15. **Dash, R.K. et al. (2003). "Computational-Mechanism Design: A Call to Arms." *IEEE IS*, 18(6), 40–47.**
    - Designing mechanisms that align selfish agent incentives.
    - Relevance: Engineering solutions for parasitic agent problems.

16. **Ranganathan, K. et al. (2004). "Incentive Mechanisms for Large Collaborative Resource Sharing." *IEEE CCGrid*.**
    - Incentive mechanisms combating free-riding in grid computing.
    - Relevance: Economic countermeasures against parasitic agents.

17. **Conitzer, V. & Sandholm, T. (2002). "Complexity of Mechanism Design." *UAI*, 103–110.**
    - Computational complexity of truthful mechanisms for selfish agents.
    - Relevance: Limits on preventing parasitic behavior through mechanism design.

18. **Hamilton, W.D. & Zuk, M. (1982). "Heritable True Fitness and Bright Birds." *Science*, 218, 384–387.**
    - Parasite-host coevolution driving signaling.
    - Relevance: Arms race dynamics between parasitic and defensive agents.

19. **Schmid-Hempel, P. (2011). *Evolutionary Parasitology*. Oxford UP.**
    - Host-parasite evolutionary dynamics.
    - Relevance: Rich analogies for parasitic agent dynamics.

20. **Shoham, Y. & Leyton-Brown, K. (2008). *Multiagent Systems*. Cambridge UP.**
    - Comprehensive MAS including adversarial settings.
    - Relevance: Theoretical foundations for exploitation dynamics.
