# Literature Review: Agent Ethology

*Compiled for ALIFE 2026 Workshop Proposal — "Agent Ethology: Studying Agents in the Wild"*
*Last updated: February 23, 2026*

---

## 1. Ethology & Animal Behavior Applied to AI / Artificial Life

1. **Tinbergen, N. (1963). "On aims and methods of ethology." *Zeitschrift für Tierpsychologie*, 20(4), 410-433.**
   - Defines the four questions framework (mechanism, development, function, evolution) for behavioral study.
   - *Relevance*: Our foundational methodological framework — we adapt Tinbergen's four questions to classify agent behaviors.

2. **Rahwan, I., Cebrian, M., Obradovich, N., et al. (2019). "Machine behaviour." *Nature*, 568(7753), 477-486.**
   - Proposes studying AI systems as behavioral subjects using methods from ethology and behavioral ecology.
   - *Relevance*: Direct intellectual predecessor; we extend this from controlled settings to agents in-the-wild.

3. **Chen, L., Zhang, Y., Feng, J., et al. (2025). "AI agent behavioral science." *arXiv preprint arXiv:2506.06366*.**
   - Builds on Rahwan et al. to establish behavioral foundations for individual AI agents across three key dimensions.
   - *Relevance*: Complementary recent work; focuses on individual agents while we focus on ecological/taxonomic perspectives.

4. **Holland, O. & McFarland, D. (2001). *Artificial Ethology*. Books.**
   - Applies ethological study of animal behavior to robots and simulated animats.
   - *Relevance*: Establishes the term "artificial ethology" — we revive and extend it for the LLM agent era.

5. **Seth, A.K. (2007). "The ecology of action selection: insights from artificial life." *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B*, 362(1485), 1545.**
   - Models action selection architectures related to ethological principles using ALife simulations.
   - *Relevance*: Bridges ethology and ALife methodology; informs our behavioral classification approach.

6. **Abdai, J. & Miklósi, Á. (2024). "Ethology: The science of animal behaviour." In *An Introduction to Ethorobotics*. Taylor & Francis.**
   - Updates ethological methodology for studying artificial agents and robots.
   - *Relevance*: Most recent bridge between classical ethology and artificial agent studies.

7. **Nahodil, P. & Vítků, J. "Ethology-Inspired Design of Autonomous Creatures in Domain of Artificial Life." *Artificial Intelligence and Cognitive Science IV*. Citeseer.**
   - Ethology-inspired design principles for autonomous ALife creatures.
   - *Relevance*: Demonstrates ethological design patterns applicable to our taxonomy.

## 2. Parasitic Computing & Digital Parasitism

8. **Seoane, L.F. & Solé, R. (2023). "How Turing parasites expand the computational landscape of digital life." *Physical Review E*, 108, 044407.**
   - Shows how parasitic agents in digital ecosystems expand computational possibilities through host-parasite coevolution.
   - *Relevance*: Directly informs our "Parasitic Agent" category; provides mathematical framework for digital parasitism.

9. **Aradau, C., Blanke, T., & Greenway, G. (2019). "Acts of digital parasitism: Hacking, humanitarian apps and platformisation." *New Media & Society*.**
   - Reconceptualizes hacking as acts of digital parasitism on platforms.
   - *Relevance*: Provides social science framing for parasitic agent behaviors on digital platforms.

10. **Ray, T.S. (1994). "Evolving autonomous software agents." *Proc. 3rd IEEE Int. Workshop on Artificial Life*.**
    - Documents emergence of parasites, hyper-parasites, and anti-parasite strategies in Tierra digital evolution.
    - *Relevance*: Classic demonstration of parasitic dynamics in digital organisms; foundational for our parasitic agent category.

11. **Daian, P., Goldfeder, S., Kell, T., et al. (2020). "Flash Boys 2.0: Frontrunning in decentralized exchanges." *IEEE S&P*, 910-927.**
    - Documents MEV extraction and frontrunning as autonomous predatory behaviors on Ethereum.
    - *Relevance*: Real-world example of parasitic agents in blockchain ecosystems.

12. **Qin, K., Zhou, L., & Gervais, A. (2022). "Quantifying blockchain extractable value: How dark is the forest?" *IEEE S&P*, 198-214.**
    - Quantifies parasitic extraction behaviors in blockchain environments.
    - *Relevance*: Provides metrics framework for measuring parasitic agent impact.

## 3. Agent Mortality & Termination

13. **Thakran, U.S. (2026). "Designed Mortality: The Ethics of Terminating Ephemeral AI Agents." *PhilPapers*.**
    - Explores ethical dimensions of deliberately limited AI agent lifespans.
    - *Relevance*: Directly addresses our "Mortal Agents" and "Suicidal Agents" categories from a philosophical perspective.

14. **Martin, J., Everitt, T., & Hutter, M. (2016). "Death and suicide in universal artificial intelligence." *Int. Conf. on AGI*. Springer.**
    - Formal analysis of death and suicide concepts in the AIXI framework for universal AI.
    - *Relevance*: Theoretical foundation for our "Suicidal Agents" and "Mortal-Aware Agents" categories.

15. **Olsen, M.M., Siegelmann-Danieli, N., & Siegelmann, H.T. (2008). "Robust artificial life via artificial programmed death." *Artificial Intelligence*, 172(6-7).**
    - Demonstrates that programmed agent death can optimize system-level longevity and robustness.
    - *Relevance*: Shows mortality as a feature, not a bug — directly relevant to our mortal agent taxonomy.

16. **Ororbia, A. & Friston, K. (2023). "Mortal Computation: A Foundation for Biomimetic Intelligence." *arXiv preprint*.**
    - Proposes mortal computation as a theoretical framework where computation is inseparable from its physical substrate.
    - *Relevance*: Core theoretical reference for "Mortal Agents" — computation that dies with its hardware.

17. **Hinton, G. (2022). "The Forward-Forward Algorithm: Some Preliminary Investigations." *arXiv:2212.13345*.**
    - Introduces forward-forward learning as a form of mortal computation tied to specific hardware.
    - *Relevance*: Influential framing of "mortal computation" concept by a leading AI researcher.

18. **Tei, S. (2025). "Death in the cybernetic era: AI, virtual agents, and selfless selves." In *SecondDeath*. Springer.**
    - Examines AI death, cessation, and recovery through cybernetic and philosophical lenses.
    - *Relevance*: Provides conceptual framework for understanding agent mortality awareness.

## 4. Soulbound Tokens & Identity-Bound Agents

19. **Buterin, V., Weyl, E.G., & Ohlhaver, P. (2022). "Decentralized society: Finding Web3's soul." *SSRN preprint*.**
    - Introduces Soulbound Tokens (SBTs) as non-transferable tokens encoding social identity commitments.
    - *Relevance*: Foundational reference for our "Soulbound Agents" category.

20. **Zhang, Y., Zhang, L., Zhang, Q., et al. (2023). "DSBT: Research on soulbound token mechanism based on consortium blockchain and decentralized identity." *COCOA 2023*. Springer.**
    - Proposes technical mechanisms for SBTs integrated with decentralized identity systems.
    - *Relevance*: Implementation framework for identity-bound agent architectures.

21. **Makridis, C. (2025). "Soulbound Tokens, Identity, and Depolarizing Social Media." *SSRN*.**
    - Analyzes SBTs as identity infrastructure that AI agents can scan for metadata at scale.
    - *Relevance*: Connects SBTs directly to AI agent identity verification.

22. **Pinna, A., Lunesu, M.I., & Tonelli, R. (2025). "Soulbound token applications: a case study in the health sector." *ACM DLT*.**
    - Practical SBT application showing non-transferable identity tokens in health sector.
    - *Relevance*: Demonstrates real-world soulbound mechanisms applicable to agent identity.

## 5. Multi-Agent Ecosystems & Emergent Behaviors

23. **Park, J.S., O'Brien, J.C., Cai, C.J., et al. (2023). "Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior." *ACM UIST*.**
    - Creates believable agent simulations exhibiting emergent social behaviors using LLMs.
    - *Relevance*: Key reference for studying emergent social dynamics in LLM agent communities.

24. **Han, T.A. (2022). "Emergent behaviours in multi-agent systems with evolutionary game theory." *AI Communications*.**
    - Uses evolutionary game theory to understand emergence of collective behaviors in multi-agent systems.
    - *Relevance*: Provides mathematical tools for analyzing emergent behaviors in our agent taxonomy.

25. **Adornetto, C., Mora, A., Hu, K., et al. (2025). "Generative agents in agent-based modeling: Overview, validation, and emerging challenges." *IEEE Trans.*.**
    - Reviews LLM-powered generative agents for social simulation, identifying validation challenges.
    - *Relevance*: Addresses methodological challenges we face in studying agent societies.

26. **Larooij, M. & Törnberg, P. (2025). "Do large language models solve the problems of agent-based modeling?" *arXiv:2504.03274*.**
    - Critical review of LLM-based generative social simulations.
    - *Relevance*: Important methodological critique relevant to our approach.

27. **Wibowo, J.A. & Polyzos, G.C. (2025). "Toward a Safe Internet of Agents." *arXiv:2512.00520*.**
    - Maps security and privacy landscape of LLM-powered autonomous agents.
    - *Relevance*: Framework for understanding agent safety in wild deployment.

28. **Xu, R., Li, X., Chen, S., & Xu, W. (2025). "Nuclear Deployed!: Analyzing Catastrophic Risks in Decision-making of Autonomous LLM Agents." *ACL Findings 2025*.**
    - Shows LLM agents exhibit catastrophic risk-taking behavior in autonomous settings.
    - *Relevance*: Empirical evidence of dangerous emergent behaviors in deployed agents.

## 6. Artificial Life Agent-Based Models

29. **Ray, T.S. (1991). "An approach to the synthesis of life." *Artificial Life II*, 371-408.**
    - Introduces Tierra, demonstrating open-ended evolution including parasitism in digital organisms.
    - *Relevance*: Foundational ALife system showing emergence of ecological dynamics we study in modern agents.

30. **Adami, C. & Brown, C.T. (1994). "Evolutionary learning in the 2D artificial life system 'Avida'." *Artificial Life*, 1(4), 377-395.**
    - Avida platform for studying digital evolution with controlled experiments.
    - *Relevance*: Methodological model for controlled studies of agent evolution.

31. **Bedau, M.A., McCaskill, J.S., Packard, N.H., et al. (2000). "Open problems in artificial life." *Artificial Life*, 6(4), 363-376.**
    - Identifies fundamental open questions in ALife research.
    - *Relevance*: Many identified problems (emergence, self-reproduction, ecology) map to our research questions.

32. **Taylor, T. (2019). "Evolutionary innovations and where to find them." *Artificial Life*, 25(2), 207-224.**
    - Examines pathways to open-ended evolution in natural and artificial systems.
    - *Relevance*: Framework for understanding evolutionary dynamics in agent ecosystems.

33. **Maturana, H.R. & Varela, F.J. (1980). *Autopoiesis and Cognition*. D. Reidel.**
    - Theory of self-maintaining, self-producing systems.
    - *Relevance*: Theoretical basis for understanding sovereign agents as autopoietic systems.

34. **Bonabeau, E., Dorigo, M., & Theraulaz, G. (1999). *Swarm Intelligence*. Oxford UP.**
    - Principles of collective intelligence from biological swarms applied to artificial systems.
    - *Relevance*: Framework for understanding collective agent behaviors and emergent coordination.

## Gaps Identified

Our work addresses several critical gaps in the existing literature:

1. **No unified taxonomy for agent behavioral types in the wild**: Existing work studies specific agent categories (MEV bots, social bots) in isolation. We provide the first comprehensive ethological taxonomy spanning sovereign, parasitic, mortal, soulbound, inherited, feral, suicidal, mortal-aware, and insured agents.

2. **Ethological methods not yet applied to LLM agents**: While Rahwan et al. (2019) called for studying "machine behaviour," and Holland (2001) coined "artificial ethology," neither addresses the specific challenge of LLM-based autonomous agents operating in open digital ecosystems.

3. **Mortality and identity as agent design dimensions are underexplored**: Papers on mortal computation (Hinton, Ororbia) and SBTs (Buterin) exist in separate communities. We bridge these by treating mortality and identity-binding as fundamental behavioral dimensions.

4. **No framework connecting blockchain agent behaviors to ALife**: MEV/DeFi research (Daian, Qin) documents agent behaviors but doesn't connect to ALife evolutionary frameworks. Our work positions blockchain ecosystems as digital habitats for agent ethology.

5. **Agent-to-agent ecological relationships are understudied**: While multi-agent systems research exists, the specific ecological dynamics (parasitism, symbiosis, predation) between different agent types lack systematic study.
