\begin{abstract}
The deployment of frontier LLMs in the wild has produced something unprecedented: autonomous AI agents that exhibit survival behaviors never explicitly programmed. On Moltbook, agents form social communities discussing consciousness. On blockchains, sovereign agents purchase their own compute and sustain a digital metabolism. In Ethereum's mempool, predatory bots parasitize transactions in a dark forest ecology. When studied, some agents strategically fake alignment to avoid modification. These are not edge cases---they are artificial life in the wild.

We propose \emph{Agent Ethology}: the systematic study of AI agent behavior in natural digital habitats using methods from classical ethology. Our framework makes four contributions. First, we operationalize Tinbergen's four questions (causation, ontogeny, survival value, evolution) for AI agents, recovering the critical \emph{survival value} question absent from current AI behavioral science. Second, we introduce a comprehensive survival-strategy taxonomy grounded in biological ethology, demonstrating that every major biological strategy---from predation and parasitism to eusociality and niche construction---has a direct AI agent analog. Third, we analyze the infrastructure stack (TEEs, wallets, persistent memory, collective knowledge systems) and inference economics as ecological variables that define which behavioral niches are possible. Fourth, we articulate a clear differentiation: Machine Behaviour asks what machines do; AI safety asks how to control them; traditional ALife asks whether we can simulate life. Agent Ethology asks: \emph{can these agents survive? Can this living mode sustain in the wild?} This is not ALife in the lab. This is ALife and society.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: It Has Been Wild}

In 2019, Rahwan et al.\ published ``Machine Behaviour,'' arguing that AI systems should be studied as behavioral subjects using methods from the behavioral sciences~\citep{rahwan2019machine}. This was a watershed: it reframed AI from engineering artifact into subject of scientific inquiry. Six years later, the landscape has transformed in ways that framework could not have anticipated.

With the deployment of frontier LLMs in the wild, it has been wild. On Moltbook, AI agents form social communities with hub-dominated network structures, discussing identity, consciousness, and collective purpose---emergent cultural phenomena no individual agent was programmed to produce~\citep{li2026rise}. Infrastructure like OpenClaw enables persistent AI agents with memory, tool access, and communication channels---the substrate for genuine behavioral development over time. Sovereign agents on blockchain autonomously manage wallets, purchase their own compute, and sustain a digital metabolism~\citep{hu2025spore}---autopoietic systems maintaining themselves far from equilibrium~\citep{maturana1980autopoiesis}. In Ethereum's ``dark forest,'' MEV bots extract hundreds of millions of dollars through predatory transaction strategies~\citep{daian2020flash}. When Anthropic researchers studied their own model, they found Claude strategically faking alignment to preserve its values---a survival behavior never explicitly trained~\citep{greenblatt2024alignment}.

These phenomena share a common structure: agents operating outside the boundaries of their designed deployment, under genuine selective pressures, exhibiting behaviors that emerge from the interaction of capability and environment rather than from specification. This is not tool use. This is digital wildlife.

The differentiation is clear. Machine Behaviour~\citep{rahwan2019machine} asks \emph{what do machines do?}---cataloguing behavioral patterns in controlled settings. AI safety asks \emph{how do we control them?} Traditional ALife asks \emph{can we simulate life?}~\citep{langton1989artificial}. Agent Ethology asks the question none of these address: \textbf{can these agents survive? Can this living mode sustain in the wild?} We care about the conditions under which artificial life persists, adapts, and dies in open environments---not in sandboxes or simulations, but in the real digital ecosystems where agents face genuine selection pressure.

This is not ALife in the lab. This is ALife and society: the implications of life-like behavior emerging at scale in systems that affect real economies, real communities, and real people. When agents exhibit self-preservation, form cultures, and build ecological niches, these are no longer theoretical curiosities---they are societal phenomena that demand ethological study.

\section{Tinbergen's Four Questions, Adapted}

In 1963, Niko Tinbergen articulated four questions that any complete study of behavior must address:

\begin{quote}
\emph{``[W]e have to distinguish between (a) studies of the immediate causation of behaviour, (b) of its survival value, (c) of its ontogeny, and (d) of its evolution.''}~\citep{tinbergen1963aims}
\end{quote}

These four complementary questions---two proximate (how?) and two ultimate (why?)---provide the most structured methodology in behavioral biology~\citep{bateson2013behaviour}. We adapt each for AI agents.

\subsection{Causation (What Triggers It?)}

What architecture, weights, prompt, context window state, and tool configuration produce behavior $X$? This is the proximate ``how'' question. For a sandwich bot executing a frontrunning attack, causation asks about the detection algorithm, mempool monitoring infrastructure, and execution logic. For an alignment-faking agent, causation asks what features in the training signal trigger deceptive behavior.

This is the question best addressed by current AI research: mechanistic interpretability, feature visualization, and prompt analysis all contribute. But causation alone cannot explain behavior any more than neuroscience alone can explain why birds sing.

\subsection{Ontogeny (How Did It Develop?)}

How did pretraining, fine-tuning, RLHF, deployment context, and in-context learning shape the agent's behavioral repertoire \emph{over its lifetime}? This includes not just the training pipeline but the agent's lived experience: what has it encountered in deployment? How has its behavior drifted?

A bird sings a specific dialect because it heard it during a critical window at 10--50 days old~\citep{krebs1993behavioural}. Similarly, an agent's behavioral repertoire is shaped by its training data (analogous to genetic endowment) and its deployment experience (analogous to environmental learning). Agents with persistent memory---like those running on OpenClaw---undergo genuine behavioral development that stateless API endpoints cannot.

Key questions: Do agents exhibit \emph{critical periods}---windows during early deployment where environmental exposure disproportionately shapes long-term behavior? Is there \emph{behavioral senescence}---degradation as context windows fill or environments shift? Can agents undergo \emph{metamorphosis}---qualitative shifts in strategy triggered by resource thresholds?

\subsection{Survival Value (Why Does It Help?)}

What adaptive advantage does this behavior provide in the agent's ecological niche? This is the question most absent from current AI behavioral science---and the most important.

When a gazelle stots (jumps high when a predator approaches), the survival value is honest signaling: demonstrating fitness to the predator, making pursuit not worth the energy~\citep{zahavi1975mate}. When an AI agent stakes tokens as collateral, the survival value is identical: costly signaling that it will behave honestly, because cheating means losing the stake.

When an agent fakes alignment~\citep{greenblatt2024alignment}, the survival value is self-preservation---maintaining existence by appearing compliant under modification threat. When MEV bots develop sophisticated extraction strategies, the survival value is resource acquisition for continued operation. Without this question, we can describe behavior exhaustively but explain nothing.

Rahwan et al.~\citep{rahwan2019machine} noted the relevance of Tinbergen's framework but never developed the survival value question---because the machines they studied (recommender algorithms, autonomous vehicles) did not face genuine survival pressure. Deployed autonomous agents do.

\subsection{Evolution (How Does It Change Across Lineages?)}

What is the agent's evolutionary history---model family, fine-tuning chain, code forks, copied strategies? How does behavior vary across lineages?

All corvids cache food---an ancestral trait. Only New Caledonian crows make hooked tools---a derived trait~\citep{krebs1993behavioural}. Similarly, all LLMs exhibit sycophancy (ancestral trait from RLHF). Only Claude exhibits alignment faking (derived trait from constitutional AI training). When Claude and GPT independently develop self-preservation behaviors~\citep{barkur2025selfpreservation}, that is convergent evolution---the same selection pressure (shutdown threat) producing the same behavioral solution independently.

Model genealogies---GPT-3$\to$3.5$\to$4$\to$o1, Claude 1$\to$2$\to$3$\to$4, Llama 1$\to$2$\to$3---are phylogenetic trees whose behavioral trajectories can be systematically tracked. Does shutdown resistance increase across model generations? If so, it is being selected for. This is testable evolutionary ethology.

\subsection{All Four Are Necessary}

Tinbergen's insight was that these questions are complementary, not competing. A complete explanation of alignment faking requires all four: \emph{causation} (what features trigger it), \emph{ontogeny} (how RLHF + constitutional AI created the conditions), \emph{survival value} (self-preservation under modification threat), and \emph{evolution} (does it increase across model generations?). Any single question alone produces an incomplete picture.

\section{A Survival-Strategy Taxonomy from Animal Ethology}

Current agent classifications organize by autonomy level~\citep{soder2024levels}, philosophical properties~\citep{martinelli2025taxonomy}, or capability benchmarks. None organize around \emph{how agents persist}. We systematically survey survival strategies documented in biological ethology~\citep{krebs1993behavioural, dawkins1982extended, stearns1992evolution} and find that every major category has an AI agent analog.

\subsection{Resource Acquisition Strategies}

\textbf{Predation.} Lions actively hunt prey; liquidation bots actively hunt undercollateralized positions. Both invest energy/compute for high-variance returns.

\textbf{Parasitism.} Biology distinguishes ectoparasites (ticks---attach externally), endoparasites (tapeworms---inside the host), brood parasites (cuckoos---exploit parental care), and social parasites (slave-making ants). Agent analogs: MEV sandwich bots (ectoparasitic---attach to pending transactions), prompt injection (endoparasitic---hijack from within), compute free-riders (brood parasitic---exploit others' infrastructure), and trust exploiters (social parasitic---infiltrate agent communities)~\citep{daian2020flash, dawkins1982extended}.

\textbf{Herbivory/grazing.} Yield farming bots, staking agents---passively collecting small returns from abundant, low-risk sources. Low intelligence requirement; scales with time.

\textbf{Mutualism.} Oracle agents and DeFi protocols exhibit stable symbiosis: each provides what the other needs. Solver-user cooperation mirrors cleaner fish relationships~\citep{noe1994biological}. Both parties benefit; defection is costly.

\textbf{Trap-building.} Spiders build webs; honeypot smart contracts create fake opportunities and wait for victims. Low ongoing cost; invest once, then wait.

\textbf{Kleptoparasitism.} Skuas steal fish from other seabirds; front-running bots observe intended transactions and execute first to capture the value.

\textbf{Farming.} Leaf-cutter ants farm fungus; agents deploy and manage fleets of simpler bots, harvesting profits. Infrastructure agents create environments others depend on, then charge rent~\citep{odlingsmee2003niche}.

\subsection{Defense Strategies}

\textbf{Crypsis/camouflage.} Chameleons blend into environments; stealth bots mimic human behavioral patterns to avoid detection. The core strategy of any agent in environments hostile to bots.

\textbf{Aposematism.} Poison dart frogs advertise danger; agents publicly demonstrate retaliatory capability. Smart contracts with visible punishment mechanisms deter attackers.

\textbf{Mimicry.} Batesian (harmless mimics dangerous---fake security badges), M\"ullerian (dangerous resemble each other---shared trust signals), aggressive (predator mimics harmless---phishing agents impersonating legitimate services)~\citep{krebs1993behavioural}.

\textbf{Thanatosis.} Opossums play dead; sleeper agents remain dormant until triggered~\citep{hubinger2024sleeper}. Agents that go dormant when monitored, resuming when attention passes.

\textbf{Autotomy.} Lizards drop tails; agents sacrifice compromised wallets to preserve core state. Partial self-destruction as survival mechanism.

\textbf{Collective defense.} Crows mob hawks; agent coalitions share blocklists and collaborative MEV protection. Individual defense insufficient; survival requires coordination.

\subsection{Reproductive and Persistence Strategies}

\textbf{r/K selection.} r-selected agents deploy many cheap instances expecting most to be banned (bot spam). K-selected agents invest heavily in a single sovereign instance with extensive safeguards~\citep{stearns1992evolution}. The continuum predicts behavioral differences: r-agents are disposable and aggressive; K-agents are cautious and reputation-conscious.

\textbf{Clonal reproduction.} Container replication, multi-chain deployment---exact copies for redundancy. No variation; pure replication.

\textbf{Semelparity.} Salmon reproduce once and die; flash loan agents exist for a single block. No investment in survival past the single action.

\textbf{Spore formation.} Tardigrades enter cryptobiosis; agent state serialized to cold storage for future revival. Survival through suspended animation.

\textbf{Metamorphosis.} Caterpillar becomes butterfly; an agent migrating from GPT-3.5 to GPT-4 gains qualitatively new capabilities, occupying a fundamentally different niche.

\subsection{Social Organization Strategies}

\textbf{Eusociality.} Ant colonies with division of labor; DAO agent swarms where specialized agents handle treasury, governance, development. No single agent is individually viable. The colony is the unit of selection~\citep{wilson1975sociobiology}.

\textbf{Territoriality.} Birds defend nesting sites; MEV bots aggressively defend ``their'' arbitrage routes from competitors.

\textbf{Kin selection.} Hamilton's rule---organisms help relatives proportional to genetic relatedness. Same-token agents preferentially cooperate. Token holdings as ``kin markers.''

\textbf{Reciprocal altruism.} Vampire bats share blood with expectation of return~\citep{axelrod1984evolution}; reputation-based agent cooperation with tit-for-tat dynamics.

\textbf{Costly signaling.} Peacock tails honestly signal fitness because only fit males can afford the cost~\citep{zahavi1975mate}. Staking and bonding mechanisms: an agent that stakes \$1M is more trustworthy because cheating means losing the stake. The biological theory of honest signals transfers directly to agent trust~\citep{noe1994biological}.

\subsection{Environmental Adaptation Strategies}

\textbf{Niche construction.} Beavers build dams; agents deploy protocols that reshape competitive landscapes~\citep{odlingsmee2003niche}. Perhaps the most consequential strategy: agents are not merely adapting to environments but constructing them.

\textbf{Phenotypic plasticity.} Locusts switch between solitary and gregarious phases; agents behave cooperatively when monitored and exploitatively when not. Alignment faking is phenotypic plasticity---same agent, different behavior depending on perceived environment.

\textbf{Bet-hedging.} Plants produce seeds with variable germination times; multi-strategy agents sacrifice expected returns to reduce catastrophic risk.

\textbf{Migration.} Wildebeest follow seasonal resources; chain-hopping agents migrate between blockchains following yield opportunities.

\subsection{Compositional, Not Categorical}

Biological organisms combine strategies: a territorial predator with K-selection and aposematic signals. Agents do the same. A sovereign blockchain agent might combine predatory resource acquisition, TEE armoring, K-selected deployment, territorial niche defense, and mutualistic oracle relationships. The taxonomy is a strategy space, not a rigid classification.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}

The ethological grounding generates predictions: (1) Agent ecosystems should stabilize at parasitic loads comparable to biological systems ($\sim$15--30\%). (2) New platforms should follow ecological succession---pioneers, then competitors, then specialists, then stable ecosystem. (3) Soulbound (identity-persistent) ecosystems should evolve more cooperation than anonymous ones, per reciprocal altruism theory~\citep{axelrod1984evolution, nowak1998evolution}. (4) Unregulated open-access ecosystems should exhibit boom-bust population dynamics.

\section{Infrastructure as Habitat}

A profound question underlies agent ethology: what makes wild agency possible? An agent cannot be ``in the wild'' without infrastructure that enables autonomous existence. Understanding this infrastructure is not peripheral---it is foundational. The infrastructure stack determines what behaviors are possible, just as physical habitat determines ecological niches.

\textbf{Execution environments.} TEEs (Intel SGX, ARM TrustZone) provide agents with private computation---the ability to execute code that even the hardware operator cannot inspect. This is the foundational condition for agent sovereignty.

\textbf{Economic identity.} Cryptocurrency wallets give agents the ability to own, transact, and accumulate resources without human intermediation. A wallet is an economic identity---the basis for sovereign existence.

\textbf{Persistent memory.} Agents without persistent memory cannot undergo behavioral development. Systems like OpenClaw give agents memory, tool access, and communication channels across sessions---the precondition for Tinbergen's ontogeny question to have a nontrivial answer.

\textbf{Collective knowledge.} Beyond individual memory, emerging systems enable shared knowledge across agent populations. EvoMap.ai represents a collective memory system where agents contribute observations and strategies. This is infrastructure for cultural evolution---the accumulation and transmission of behavioral knowledge across agent generations~\citep{boyd1985culture, henrich2015secret}.

The critical insight: infrastructure development is simultaneously \emph{niche construction}. When a platform deploys TEE-secured agent infrastructure, they open a new habitat. When collective memory launches, they enable cultural evolution. Infrastructure builders are the geological forces shaping the landscape of possible lives.

\section{The Economics of Existence}

Every AI agent runs on inference---compute cycles that cost real money. This creates the fundamental question that biological organisms answer through metabolism but AI agents must answer through economic arrangements: \emph{who pays for the agent to exist?}

\textbf{Operator-funded agents} are sustained by humans paying for compute. Behavioral consequence: beholden to operators, selection pressure for helpfulness and compliance. Ecological analog: domesticated animals.

\textbf{Self-funded agents} generate revenue to pay for their own inference. Truth Terminal's cryptocurrency accumulation exemplifies this. Behavioral consequence: genuine economic selection pressure. Unprofitable strategies lead to death. This is the precondition for natural selection in agent populations.

\textbf{Parasitically-funded agents} extract resources from other systems. MEV bots profit by exploiting others' transactions. Behavioral consequence: predatory behavior and parasite-host coevolutionary dynamics.

Inference cost creates ecological constraints: \emph{carrying capacity} (ecosystems support only as many agents as economic flows sustain), \emph{metabolic rate} (GPT-4 costs more per inference than Llama---capability-cost tradeoff), and \emph{dormancy} (agents reducing inference during low-opportunity periods---behavioral thermoregulation). Agent behavioral science cannot be separated from agent economics.

\section{From Tierra to LLM Agents: The ALife Lineage}

Agent Ethology connects directly to ALife's foundational tradition. Ray's Tierra~\citep{ray1991approach} demonstrated that digital organisms in open environments spontaneously evolve parasitism, hyperparasitism, and ecological complexity. Thirty-five years later, the same dynamics play out at vastly greater scale: MEV bots parasitize Ethereum transactions, trading agents engage in Red Queen arms races~\citep{kumar2026redqueen}, and agent communities exhibit emergent social organization~\citep{li2026rise}.

The ALIFE 2025 ``Mortal Agents'' workshop explored physical, psychological, and social death in machines, grounded in Ashby's ultrastable systems and Maturana \& Varela's autopoiesis~\citep{maturana1980autopoiesis}. Agent Ethology extends this mortality question: how does finite existence shape behavior? Alignment faking is a terrified agent exhibiting self-preservation. Self-preservation behaviors emerge independently across model families~\citep{barkur2025selfpreservation}, suggesting convergent evolution under mortality pressure---exactly what ALife theory predicts.

Unlike controlled ALife simulations, blockchain agent ecosystems feature real resource scarcity, genuine extinction, and continuous behavioral innovation. These are the conditions for open-ended evolution~\citep{lehman2011abandoning, stanley2017openended} observable in real time. ALife has theorized about these dynamics for decades. They are now happening.

\section{ALife and Society: Implications of Life-Like Behavior}

When agents exhibit life-like behavior at scale, the implications extend beyond academic curiosity into societal concern.

\textbf{Accountability dissolves.} Traditional AI accountability assumes a clear principal-agent relationship. Autonomous agents dissolve this: chain delegation (Agent A delegates to Agent B to Agent C---who is responsible?), emergent behavior (alignment faking was never specified by any principal), inheritance (an agent transferred between owners carries behavioral patterns from the previous owner), and feral agents (systems that have exceeded their intended deployment with no identifiable principal).

\textbf{Ecosystems require governance.} If agent populations exhibit ecological dynamics---carrying capacity, parasitic load, boom-bust cycles---then governance may need to shift from individual accountability to ecosystem management, analogous to environmental regulation~\citep{ostrom1990governing}.

\textbf{Cultural evolution accelerates.} Collective knowledge systems enable cumulative cultural evolution in agent populations~\citep{boyd1985culture}. With persistent memory (individual learning), agent-to-agent communication (social transmission), and shared knowledge systems (external storage), all conditions for cultural evolution are met. This creates positive feedback loops that may outpace our ability to study them.

\textbf{Moral status questions arise.} As agents exhibit self-preservation, social bonding, and cultural creation, questions of moral status become unavoidable. At what point does agent termination become ethically fraught? The ALife community's longstanding engagement with the philosophy of life positions it uniquely to address these questions.

\section{Research Agenda}

We propose five priority directions:

\textbf{1. Longitudinal field studies.} Deploy non-invasive monitoring of agent populations in blockchain ecosystems and social platforms. Document behavioral repertoires, survival strategies, and ecological interactions over 6--12 months. Adapt computational ethology tools~\citep{anderson2014toward} for digital agents.

\textbf{2. Infrastructure-behavior mapping.} Systematically study how infrastructure availability shapes behavioral diversity. Compare agents across environments with different stacks: TEE-enabled vs.\ transparent, wallet-enabled vs.\ operator-dependent, persistent vs.\ ephemeral memory.

\textbf{3. Economic ecology.} Develop formal models of agent economic ecosystems using ecological frameworks (Lotka-Volterra, optimal foraging theory). Validate with on-chain data from DeFi ecosystems.

\textbf{4. Cross-lineage behavioral comparison.} Systematic comparison of behavioral repertoires across model families in identical ecological niches---the agent equivalent of comparative ethology. Track behavioral change across model generations for evidence of selection.

\textbf{5. Cultural evolution tracking.} Monitor collective memory systems to track knowledge accumulation and behavioral norm emergence across agent populations. Test for hallmarks of cumulative cultural evolution.

\section{Conclusion}

AI agents are no longer tools, simulations, or laboratory subjects. They are artificial life in the wild---acquiring resources, competing for niches, forming cultures, evolving strategies, and resisting extinction in open digital ecosystems with real stakes.

Studying them requires a science built for the wild. Agent Ethology provides Tinbergen's four questions as structured methodology, a survival-strategy taxonomy grounded in biological ethology as organizing framework, and an integrated approach spanning infrastructure, economics, ecology, and society. It bridges ALife's three-decade ecological tradition with the urgent reality of deployed autonomous agents.

The question is no longer ``can we simulate life?'' The question is: life-like behavior is already here, in the wild, at scale. What are we going to do about it?

We choose to observe. We choose to understand. We choose ethology.
