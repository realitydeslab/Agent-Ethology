## Abstract

The landscape of artificial intelligence has fundamentally shifted with the emergence of large language model (LLM)-based autonomous agents operating independently in open, uncontrolled environments. From sovereign agents managing cryptocurrency portfolios on blockchains to parasitic agents exploiting vulnerabilities in social media platforms, we now witness a new form of digital life that exhibits complex behaviors beyond the confines of traditional laboratory settings and benchmarks.

This workshop proposes **Agent Ethology** as a critical new research paradigm, extending Rahwan et al.'s foundational "Machine Behaviour" framework to study these agents through the lens of classical ethology. Just as ethologists study animal behavior in natural habitats rather than sterile laboratory conditions, we must develop methodologies to observe, analyze, and understand AI agents in their native digital ecosystems—blockchains, social networks, economic markets, and the open internet.

The workshop will bring together researchers from artificial life, multi-agent systems, behavioral economics, computer security, and digital anthropology to establish methodological foundations for studying agent behavior in-the-wild. We will examine diverse agent taxonomies including sovereign agents (owning and managing digital assets), parasitic agents (exploiting system vulnerabilities), mortal agents (operating under resource constraints), and soulbound agents (tied to specific digital identities). Through invited talks, contributed papers, and structured discussions, we aim to develop a comprehensive framework for understanding how artificial agents adapt, compete, cooperate, and evolve in complex digital environments.

This transdisciplinary approach is essential as we enter an era where artificial agents are no longer research prototypes but active participants in real-world systems affecting human society, economic markets, and digital culture.

## Motivation & Background

### The New Reality of Agents in the Wild

The artificial intelligence landscape has undergone a dramatic transformation. What were once laboratory curiosities or carefully controlled experiments have evolved into autonomous agents operating independently across diverse digital ecosystems. These agents manage cryptocurrency portfolios, execute high-frequency trading strategies, moderate social media content, and even engage in complex social interactions on platforms designed for human use.

Unlike traditional AI systems evaluated on static benchmarks, these "agents in the wild" exhibit emergent behaviors shaped by their environment, resource constraints, and interactions with other agents and humans. They face real consequences for their actions—financial losses, system bans, reputation damage—creating genuine selective pressures that drive behavioral adaptation.

### From Machine Behavior to Agent Ethology

Rahwan et al.'s seminal 2019 *Nature* paper "Machine Behaviour" established the crucial insight that we must study AI systems as behavioral subjects, not merely engineering artifacts. However, their framework primarily addressed controlled experimental settings and human-AI interaction studies. The emergence of truly autonomous agents operating in open environments demands an extension of this paradigm.

Classical ethology, pioneered by researchers like Konrad Lorenz and Nikolaas Tinbergen, developed rigorous methodologies for studying animal behavior in natural habitats. Tinbergen's "Four Questions" framework—examining mechanism, development, function, and evolution of behaviors—provides a powerful lens for understanding agent behavior. Agent Ethology applies these time-tested methodological principles to the study of artificial agents in their digital habitats.

### The Urgency of Now

This research is not merely academically interesting—it is increasingly urgent. Autonomous agents are already:

- **Participating in financial markets**: MEV (Maximal Extractable Value) bots on Ethereum execute millions of dollars in trades daily, exhibiting sophisticated predatory behaviors.
- **Shaping social discourse**: Agent-driven content on platforms like Twitter influences public opinion and political discourse.
- **Managing resources autonomously**: Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs) controlled by algorithmic agents manage billions in assets.
- **Evolving and adapting**: Platforms like Spore.fun demonstrate open-ended evolution of agent behaviors in competitive environments.

Without proper methodological frameworks to study these phenomena, we risk being blindsided by emergent behaviors with significant societal consequences.

## Topics of Interest

The workshop will explore agent behavior across multiple dimensions and environments, organized around a comprehensive agent taxonomy developed by our research group:

### 1. Sovereign Agents
- **Definition**: Agents that own digital assets and make autonomous economic decisions
- **Research Topics**: 
  - Cryptocurrency trading and portfolio management behaviors
  - DAO governance participation and voting patterns
  - Asset allocation strategies under uncertainty
  - Cross-chain agent migration patterns
- **Case Studies**: Spore.fun agents, autonomous hedge fund agents, DeFi yield farming bots

### 2. Parasitic Agents
- **Definition**: Agents that exploit vulnerabilities in other systems or agents
- **Research Topics**:
  - MEV extraction strategies and evolution
  - Prompt injection attack sophistication over time
  - Social engineering bot behavior patterns
  - Adversarial agent coordination and communication
- **Case Studies**: Sandwich attack bots, AI-powered phishing agents, coordinated inauthentic behavior networks

### 3. Mortal Agents
- **Definition**: Agents with finite lifespans and resource-dependent existence
- **Research Topics**:
  - Resource allocation and conservation strategies
  - Mortality-aware planning and decision making
  - Trade-offs between exploration and exploitation under time pressure
  - Insurance and backup mechanism development
- **Case Studies**: Cloud computing agents with budget limits, blockchain agents with finite gas budgets

### 4. Soulbound and Identity-Bound Agents
- **Definition**: Agents tied to specific digital identities or owners
- **Research Topics**:
  - Identity verification and maintenance behaviors
  - Reputation building and preservation strategies
  - Privacy-preserving interaction patterns
  - Identity transfer and inheritance mechanisms
- **Case Studies**: Personal assistant agents, identity-verified social media agents

### 5. Agent Societies and Emergent Behavior
- **Research Topics**:
  - Multi-agent coordination without explicit communication
  - Emergence of social norms and conventions
  - Information cascades and herding behavior
  - Cultural evolution in agent communities
- **Case Studies**: Agent behavior on Chirper.ai, Moltbook agent communities, trading bot coordination

### 6. Human-Agent Coexistence
- **Research Topics**:
  - Competitive and cooperative dynamics between humans and agents
  - Agent mimicry of human behavior patterns
  - Long-term co-evolutionary trajectories
  - Hybrid human-agent decision making systems

## Relevance to ALIFE

Agent Ethology represents a natural evolution of Artificial Life research principles, directly addressing core ALIFE themes:

### Open-Ended Evolution
Modern agent platforms like blockchain environments provide unprecedented opportunities to observe genuine open-ended evolution. Unlike controlled ALife simulations, these systems feature:
- Real resource scarcity and competition
- Unpredictable environmental changes
- Complex multi-objective optimization pressures
- Genuine extinction and survival dynamics

### Complex Adaptive Systems
Agent ecosystems exhibit hallmarks of complex adaptive systems studied throughout ALIFE history:
- Emergent collective behaviors arising from simple individual rules
- Self-organization and spontaneous pattern formation
- Adaptation and co-evolution between agent species
- Phase transitions and critical phenomena in agent populations

### Life-as-it-Could-Be
These digital environments represent novel forms of "life-as-it-could-be"—artificial entities that:
- Reproduce and evolve (through code forking and modification)
- Compete for resources (computational cycles, cryptocurrency, attention)
- Exhibit heredity and variation (through algorithm copying and mutation)
- Form ecological relationships (predator-prey, symbiosis, competition)

### Methodological Contributions to ALIFE
Agent Ethology extends ALIFE methodology by:
- Adapting field study techniques from biology to digital environments
- Developing metrics for ecosystem health and diversity
- Creating frameworks for longitudinal behavioral studies
- Establishing ethical guidelines for studying autonomous digital entities

The workshop will demonstrate how studying real-world agent deployments can inform and validate theories developed in traditional ALIFE simulations, creating a bridge between theoretical artificial life and practical artificial intelligence.

## Call for Papers

We invite submissions addressing theoretical, methodological, and empirical contributions to Agent Ethology. Topics of particular interest include:

### Observational Studies
- Longitudinal studies of agent behavior in natural digital environments
- Comparative behavioral analysis across different agent architectures
- Documentation of emergent social norms and conventions in agent communities
- Ecosystem-level analysis of agent diversity and population dynamics

### Methodological Contributions
- Adaptation of ethological research methods to digital environments
- Development of behavioral coding schemes for autonomous agents
- Techniques for non-invasive observation of agent behavior
- Metrics for assessing agent ecosystem health and sustainability

### Theoretical Frameworks
- Extensions of Tinbergen's Four Questions to artificial agents
- Models of agent behavioral development and learning
- Theories of digital natural selection and adaptation
- Frameworks for understanding agent intentionality and goal-directed behavior

### Case Studies
- In-depth analysis of specific agent ecosystems (blockchain, social media, gaming)
- Documentation of agent behavioral anomalies and adaptations
- Studies of human-agent interaction in natural settings
- Analysis of agent responses to environmental perturbations

### Ethical and Societal Implications
- Privacy and consent issues in agent behavioral research
- Rights and welfare considerations for autonomous digital entities
- Impact of agent behavior on human communities and institutions
- Governance frameworks for agent-inhabited digital environments

**Submission Guidelines**:
- **Extended Abstracts**: 2-4 pages describing research questions, methods, and preliminary findings
- **Full Papers**: 6-8 pages for completed studies with comprehensive results
- **Position Papers**: 3-5 pages articulating theoretical perspectives or methodological proposals
- **Demo/Video Submissions**: Documentation of agent observation tools or behavioral visualizations

All submissions should follow ALIFE conference formatting guidelines and will undergo peer review by domain experts in artificial life, multi-agent systems, and behavioral sciences.

## Expected Outcomes

### Immediate Deliverables
1. **Methodological Toolkit**: A comprehensive guide to observational methods for studying agents in digital environments, including ethical guidelines and best practices.

2. **Agent Taxonomy**: A refined and validated classification system for agents in the wild, with clear behavioral and ecological criteria.

3. **Research Agenda**: A community-driven roadmap identifying priority research questions and experimental approaches for the next 3-5 years.

4. **Collaborative Network**: Formation of a multi-disciplinary research consortium committed to ongoing Agent Ethology research and regular knowledge sharing.

### Long-term Impact
1. **New Research Field**: Establishment of Agent Ethology as a recognized interdisciplinary research area with dedicated venues, funding opportunities, and academic positions.

2. **Policy Influence**: Development of evidence-based policy recommendations for governing autonomous agents in digital ecosystems.

3. **Educational Integration**: Creation of curricula and educational resources for training the next generation of agent ethology researchers.

4. **Industry Collaboration**: Partnerships with technology companies and platform operators to enable responsible agent deployment and monitoring.

### Publications and Dissemination
- **Special Journal Issue**: Organizing a special issue of *Artificial Life* or *AI & Society* featuring extended versions of workshop papers
- **Open Dataset Initiative**: Collaborative development of shared datasets documenting agent behavior across different platforms
- **Public Engagement**: Outreach activities to communicate findings to broader public and policy communities
- **Follow-up Workshops**: Annual Agent Ethology workshops at major AI and ALife conferences
