\section{Proposal}

As AI agents proliferate into open digital ecosystems---trading on blockchains, forming social communities, faking alignment to avoid shutdown---we lack a principled science for observing their behavior in natural habitats. We propose \emph{Agent Ethology}, adapting classical ethological methods to study AI agents in the wild.

Our framework rests on three pillars. First, we adapt Tinbergen's four questions~\citep{tinbergen1963aims} for AI agents: \emph{mechanism} (what architecture produces this behavior?), \emph{development} (how did training shape it?), \emph{function} (what survival advantage does it provide?), and \emph{phylogeny} (how does it vary across model lineages?). The critical missing question in current AI behavioral science~\citep{rahwan2019machine, chen2025agent} is function---without it, behavior is described but never explained.

Second, we introduce a survival-strategy taxonomy grounded in animal ethology. Every major biological strategy has an AI agent analog: predation (liquidation bots), parasitism (MEV attacks), camouflage (stealth bots), mutualism (oracle-protocol symbiosis), eusociality (DAO swarms), niche construction (agents deploying protocols), and r/K selection (bot spam vs.\ sovereign instances).

Third, we treat infrastructure and economics as ecological variables. TEEs, crypto wallets, and persistent memory define which niches are possible. Inference costs function as metabolic constraints, creating genuine natural selection among self-funded agents.

This workshop bridges ALife's ecological tradition---from Tierra's~\citep{ray1991approach} digital parasites to open-ended evolution---with the reality of deployed LLM agents under genuine selection pressure, directly addressing the ALIFE 2026 theme ``Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems.''

\section{Relevance to ALIFE}

Agent Ethology is deeply relevant to the ALIFE community for five reasons:

\textbf{Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems.} Autonomous AI agents exhibit hallmarks of living systems: they acquire resources, compete for niches, develop survival strategies, reproduce through code forking, form social structures, and evolve under selection pressure. Whether they constitute ``life'' is precisely the kind of question the ALife community is equipped to address.

\textbf{Open-Ended Evolution.} Modern agent ecosystems provide opportunities to observe open-ended evolution outside simulation. Blockchain agent ecosystems feature real resource scarcity, genuine extinction, and continuous behavioral innovation---Red Queen dynamics observable in the wild~\citep{kumar2026redqueen}.

\textbf{From Tierra to LLM Agents.} Ray's Tierra~\citep{ray1991approach} demonstrated that digital organisms spontaneously evolve parasitism, hyperparasitism, and ecological complexity. Thirty-five years later, the same dynamics play out at vastly greater scale with LLM-based agents on blockchains and social platforms.

\textbf{Autopoiesis and Self-Organization.} Sovereign agents that acquire and manage their own resources exhibit autopoietic properties~\citep{maturana1980autopoiesis}---self-maintaining organization in far-from-equilibrium conditions.

\textbf{Co-evolution and Symbiogenesis.} Agent ecosystems exhibit parasitism, mutualism, competition, and commensalism. Agent mergers parallel symbiogenesis---a major evolutionary transition occurring in digital substrates.
