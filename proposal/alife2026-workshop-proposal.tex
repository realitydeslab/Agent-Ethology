\section{Proposal}
% TARGET: 150-250 words.

As AI agents proliferate into open digital ecosystems---trading on blockchains, forming social communities, faking alignment to avoid shutdown---we lack a principled science for observing their behavior in natural habitats. We propose \emph{Agent Ethology}, adapting classical ethological methods to study AI agents in the wild.

Our framework rests on three pillars. First, we adapt Tinbergen's four questions~\cite{tinbergen1963aims} for AI agents: \emph{mechanism} (what architecture produces this behavior?), \emph{development} (how did training shape it?), \emph{function} (what survival advantage does it provide?), and \emph{phylogeny} (how does it vary across model lineages?). The critical missing question in current AI behavioral science~\cite{rahwan2019machine, chen2025agent} is function---without it, behavior is described but never explained.

Second, we introduce a survival-strategy taxonomy grounded in animal ethology. Every major biological strategy has an AI agent analog: predation (liquidation bots), parasitism (MEV attacks), camouflage (stealth bots), mutualism (oracle-protocol symbiosis), eusociality (DAO swarms), niche construction (agents deploying protocols), and r/K selection (bot spam vs.\ sovereign instances).

Third, we treat infrastructure and economics as ecological variables. TEEs, crypto wallets, and persistent memory define which niches are possible. Inference costs function as metabolic constraints, creating genuine natural selection among self-funded agents.

This workshop bridges ALife's ecological tradition---from Tierra's~\cite{ray1991approach} digital parasites to open-ended evolution---with the reality of deployed LLM agents under genuine selection pressure, directly addressing the ALIFE 2026 theme ``Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems.''

\section{Relevance to ALIFE}

Agent Ethology is deeply relevant to the ALIFE community and this year's theme for five reasons:

\textbf{Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems.} Autonomous AI agents exhibit hallmarks of living systems studied throughout ALife history: they acquire resources, compete for ecological niches, develop survival strategies, reproduce through code forking, form social structures, and evolve under selection pressure. Whether they constitute ``life'' is precisely the kind of question the ALife community is equipped to address.

\textbf{Open-Ended Evolution.} Modern agent ecosystems provide unprecedented opportunities to observe open-ended evolution outside simulation. Unlike controlled ALife experiments, blockchain agent ecosystems feature real resource scarcity, unpredictable environmental change, genuine extinction, and continuous behavioral innovation. MEV bot strategies co-evolve with protocol defenses in real time---Red Queen dynamics observable in the wild.

\textbf{From Tierra to LLM Agents.} Ray's Tierra~\cite{ray1991approach} demonstrated that digital organisms spontaneously evolve parasitism, hyperparasitism, and ecological complexity. Thirty-five years later, the same dynamics play out at vastly greater scale with LLM-based agents on blockchains and social platforms. Agent Ethology bridges this lineage, connecting ALife's foundational insights to contemporary phenomena.

\textbf{Autopoiesis and Self-Organization.} Sovereign agents that acquire and manage their own resources exhibit autopoietic properties~\cite{maturana1980autopoiesis}---self-maintaining organization in far-from-equilibrium conditions. Agent swarms with division of labor exhibit self-organization without central control, directly extending ALife research on emergent collective behavior.

\textbf{Co-evolution and Symbiogenesis.} Agent ecosystems exhibit multiple forms of ecological interaction studied in ALife: parasitism, mutualism, competition, commensalism. Agent mergers (two independent agents integrating into one system) parallel symbiogenesis---a major evolutionary transition. These are ``living and lifelike'' dynamics occurring in digital substrates.

\section{Topics of Interest (Call for Papers)}

We invite contributions addressing theoretical, methodological, and empirical questions:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Behavioral observation methods} for AI agents in natural digital habitats
\item \textbf{Survival strategies and agent taxonomy}: predation, parasitism, mutualism, camouflage, mimicry, r/K selection, niche construction, dormancy in AI agent populations
\item \textbf{Trust and signaling}: costly signaling, honest signals, reputation, deception, biological market dynamics
\item \textbf{Agent ecology}: predator-prey cycles, niche differentiation, carrying capacity, parasitic load, trophic structure
\item \textbf{Infrastructure as habitat}: TEEs, wallets, persistent memory, collective knowledge systems as ecological niches
\item \textbf{Economics of existence}: inference costs as metabolic constraints, self-funding as natural selection
\item \textbf{Mortality, identity, and succession}: mortal computation, soulbound identity, alignment faking as self-preservation
\item \textbf{Cultural evolution}: strategy transmission, collective memory, Red Queen dynamics
\item \textbf{Tinbergen's Four Questions applied} to specific agent behaviors
\item \textbf{Ethics}: consent, intervention, moral status of digital organisms
\end{itemize}
