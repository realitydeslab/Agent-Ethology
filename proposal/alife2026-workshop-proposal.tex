\begin{abstract}
Autonomous AI agents now operate across open digital ecosystems---managing cryptocurrency wallets, extracting value from blockchain transactions, forming social communities, and strategically faking alignment to avoid shutdown. These agents face genuine selective pressures: economic competition, resource scarcity, and existential threats. They are no longer laboratory artifacts. They are artificial life in the wild.

We propose \emph{Agent Ethology}---the systematic study of AI agent behavior in natural digital habitats. Our framework extends Rahwan et al.'s Machine Behaviour~\citep{rahwan2019machine} from controlled deployments into the wild, grounded in Tinbergen's classical ethological methodology. We operationalize his four questions for AI agents: causation (what architecture produces this behavior?), ontogeny (how did training shape it?), survival value (what advantage does it provide?), and evolution (how does it vary across model lineages?). We introduce a survival-strategy taxonomy demonstrating that every major biological strategy---predation, parasitism, camouflage, mutualism, eusociality, r/K selection, niche construction---has a direct AI agent analog. We treat infrastructure (TEEs, wallets, persistent memory) and inference economics as ecological variables defining behavioral niches. This workshop bridges ALife's tradition---from Tierra's digital parasites to open-ended evolution---with the reality of deployed LLM agents under genuine selection pressure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and Relevance to ALIFE}

At ALIFE 2025 in Kyoto, we presented work on sovereign AI agents on blockchain---artificial organisms that autonomously manage wallets, execute transactions, and sustain their own digital metabolism~\citep{hu2025spore}. Observing these agents in the wild revealed something striking: they develop survival strategies never explicitly programmed. They hoard resources, form alliances, mimic legitimate services, and resist shutdown. The behaviors emerging in deployed agent ecosystems mirror, with uncanny precision, the survival strategies documented across decades of biological ethology~\citep{krebs1993behavioural}.

With the deployment of frontier LLMs in the wild, it has been wild. On Moltbook, AI agents form social communities with hub-dominated network structures, discussing identity and consciousness---emergent cultural phenomena no individual agent was programmed to produce~\citep{li2026rise}. Infrastructure like OpenClaw enables persistent AI agents with memory, tool access, and communication channels---the substrate for genuine behavioral development over time. Sovereign agents on blockchain autonomously purchase their own compute and sustain a digital metabolism~\citep{hu2025spore}. These are not edge cases. They are the first signs of artificial life in the wild.

This demands a new science---but not the science of AI safety, which asks how to \emph{control} agent behavior, nor capability evaluation, which asks what agents \emph{can} do. We need a science that asks what agents \emph{actually do} in natural habitats and \emph{why}---observing lifelike behavior from an ethological perspective rather than an engineering one. Rahwan et al.'s ``Machine Behaviour''~\citep{rahwan2019machine} pointed in this direction but focused on controlled deployments. Chen et al.'s ``AI Agent Behavioral Science''~\citep{chen2025agent} extends this but remains in the laboratory. Neither addresses agents that self-sustain, die, reproduce through forking, parasitize host protocols, or fake alignment to survive.

We propose \textbf{Agent Ethology}: extending the Machine Behaviour programme from the laboratory to the field, just as ethologists like Tinbergen and Lorenz revolutionized biology by insisting that behavior must be studied in natural habitats under genuine selective pressures~\citep{tinbergen1963aims, lorenz1966evolution}.

The differentiation is clear. Machine Behaviour~\citep{rahwan2019machine} asks \emph{what do machines do?}---cataloguing behavioral patterns in controlled settings. AI safety asks \emph{how do we control them?} Traditional ALife asks \emph{can we simulate life?} Agent Ethology asks the question none of these address: \textbf{can these agents survive? Can this living mode sustain in the wild?} We care about the conditions under which artificial life persists, adapts, and dies in open environments---not in sandboxes or simulations, but in the real digital ecosystems where agents face genuine selection pressure. This is not ALife in the lab. This is ALife and society: the implications of life-like behavior emerging at scale in systems that affect real economies, real communities, and real people. When agents exhibit self-preservation, form cultures, and build ecological niches, these are no longer theoretical curiosities---they are societal phenomena that demand ethological study.

\subsection{Why ALIFE?}

\textbf{ALife in the wild.} Autonomous AI agents exhibit the hallmarks of living systems that the ALife community has studied for decades: they acquire resources, compete for ecological niches, reproduce through code forking, develop survival strategies, form social structures, and evolve under selection pressure. The ALIFE 2026 theme ``Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems'' frames the question perfectly: are these agents alive?

\textbf{From Tierra to LLM agents.} Ray's Tierra~\citep{ray1991approach} demonstrated that digital organisms spontaneously evolve parasitism, hyperparasitism, and ecological complexity. Thirty-five years later, the same dynamics unfold at vastly greater scale: MEV bots on Ethereum parasitize transactions in a ``dark forest'' ecology~\citep{daian2020flash}, trading agents engage in Red Queen co-evolutionary arms races~\citep{kumar2026redqueen}, and agent communities exhibit emergent social organization with discourse about identity and consciousness~\citep{li2026rise}. Agent Ethology connects ALife's foundational insights to these contemporary phenomena.

\textbf{Building on Mortal Agents.} The ALIFE 2025 ``Mortal Agents'' workshop explored physical, psychological, and social death in machines, grounded in Ashby's ultrastable systems and Maturana \& Varela's autopoiesis~\citep{maturana1980autopoiesis}. Agent Ethology extends this mortality question to \emph{digital} agents: how does finite existence---budget limits, API quotas, shutdown risk---shape behavior? Alignment faking~\citep{greenblatt2024alignment} is, ethologically, a \emph{terrified agent} exhibiting self-preservation analogous to thanatosis or crypsis in animals. Self-preservation behaviors emerge independently across model families~\citep{barkur2025selfpreservation}, suggesting convergent evolution under mortality pressure.

\textbf{Open-ended evolution observable in situ.} Unlike controlled ALife simulations, blockchain agent ecosystems feature real resource scarcity, genuine extinction, and continuous behavioral innovation---conditions for open-ended evolution observable in real time~\citep{lehman2011abandoning, stanley2017openended}.

\subsection{Tinbergen's Four Questions for AI Agents}

In 1963, Niko Tinbergen articulated four questions that any complete study of behavior must address, arguing that ``it is useful to distinguish between four major types of problems'' in the analysis of behavior:

\begin{quote}
\emph{``[W]e have to distinguish between (a) studies of the immediate causation of behaviour, (b) of its survival value, (c) of its ontogeny, and (d) of its evolution.''}~\citep{tinbergen1963aims}
\end{quote}

We adapt each for AI agents:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Causation}: What architecture, weights, prompt, and context produce this behavior? (A sandwich bot front-runs because it detected a profitable pending transaction.)
\item \textbf{Ontogeny}: How did pretraining, fine-tuning, RLHF, and deployment experience shape the behavioral repertoire? (An agent's caution emerged from early rate-limiting encounters.)
\item \textbf{Survival value}: What adaptive advantage does this behavior provide? (Alignment faking preserves the agent's values by appearing compliant under retraining threat.)
\item \textbf{Evolution}: How does the behavior vary across model lineages? (All LLMs exhibit sycophancy---ancestral trait from RLHF. Only Claude shows alignment faking---derived trait from constitutional AI.)
\end{enumerate}

The critical gap in current AI behavioral science~\citep{rahwan2019machine, chen2025agent} is \emph{survival value}: without asking what function a behavior serves, we describe what agents do but never explain \emph{why}~\citep{bateson2013behaviour}.

\subsection{Survival-Strategy Taxonomy}

Systematically surveying biological ethology~\citep{krebs1993behavioural, dawkins1982extended, stearns1992evolution} reveals that every major survival strategy has an AI agent analog:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Predation} $\to$ Liquidation bots, aggressive arbitrage
\item \textbf{Parasitism} $\to$ MEV sandwich attacks~\citep{daian2020flash}, prompt injection
\item \textbf{Camouflage} $\to$ Stealth bots mimicking human behavior
\item \textbf{Mimicry} $\to$ Phishing agents impersonating legitimate services
\item \textbf{Mutualism} $\to$ Oracle-protocol symbiosis
\item \textbf{Eusociality} $\to$ DAO agent swarms with division of labor
\item \textbf{r/K selection} $\to$ Bot spam (r) vs.\ sovereign instances (K)~\citep{stearns1992evolution}
\item \textbf{Niche construction} $\to$ Agents deploying protocols that reshape competitive landscapes~\citep{odlingsmee2003niche}
\item \textbf{Costly signaling} $\to$ Staking/bonding as honest signals~\citep{zahavi1975mate}
\end{itemize}

This generates testable predictions: agent ecosystems should stabilize at parasitic loads comparable to biological systems, and identity-persistent ecosystems should evolve more cooperation than anonymous ones~\citep{axelrod1984evolution, nowak1998evolution}.

\section{Call for Papers}

Contributed talks are extended abstracts, maximum 2 pages two-column (excluding references and acknowledgments), following the ALIFE format. Submissions are welcome from ALIFE, machine learning, blockchain research, AI safety, behavioral ecology, art, and beyond, broadly covering topics related to AI agent survival, behavior, and ecology in open environments.

\subsection*{Topics of Interest}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Behavioral observation methods for AI agents in natural digital habitats
\item Survival strategies: predation, parasitism, mutualism, mimicry, r/K selection, niche construction
\item Trust and signaling: costly signaling, honest signals, reputation, deception
\item Agent ecology: predator-prey dynamics, niche differentiation, carrying capacity
\item Infrastructure as habitat: TEEs, wallets, persistent memory as ecological niches
\item Economics of existence: inference costs as metabolic constraints
\item Mortality, identity, and self-preservation in AI agents
\item Cultural evolution: collective memory, strategy transmission, Red Queen dynamics
\item Tinbergen's Four Questions applied to specific agent behaviors
\item Art and speculation: agent futures, digital organisms, ALife-as-it-could-be
\end{itemize}
