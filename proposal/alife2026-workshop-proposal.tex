\section{Proposal}

Autonomous AI agents now operate independently across open digital ecosystems---managing cryptocurrency portfolios, extracting value from blockchain transactions, forming social communities, and even faking alignment to avoid shutdown. These agents face genuine selective pressures: economic competition, resource scarcity, social reputation, and existential threats. They are no longer laboratory artifacts. They are digital wildlife.

Yet we lack a principled science for observing and understanding their behavior. Rahwan et al.'s ``Machine Behaviour''~\cite{rahwan2019machine} established that AI systems should be studied as behavioral subjects, but focused on controlled deployments (recommender algorithms, autonomous vehicles). Chen et al.'s ``AI Agent Behavioral Science''~\cite{chen2025agent} extends this but remains in the laboratory tradition.

We propose \textbf{Agent Ethology}---a framework that adapts classical ethological methods to study AI agents in natural digital habitats, extending the Machine Behaviour programme into the wild. Just as ethologists like Tinbergen and Lorenz revolutionized biology by studying animals in nature rather than cages, we argue the same disciplinary shift is now essential for AI.

The framework rests on three pillars:

\textbf{(1) Tinbergen's Four Questions, adapted for AI agents.} Tinbergen's~\cite{tinbergen1963aims} four complementary questions---mechanism, development, function, and evolution---provide the most structured methodology in behavioral biology. We adapt them: \emph{mechanism} (what architecture, prompt, and context produce this behavior?), \emph{development} (how did training, fine-tuning, and deployment experience shape it?), \emph{function} (what survival advantage does it provide?), and \emph{phylogeny} (how does it vary across model lineages?). The critical gap in current AI behavioral science is the function question---without asking what survival value a behavior provides, we can describe what agents do but never explain \emph{why}.

\textbf{(2) A survival-strategy taxonomy grounded in animal ethology.} We systematically survey biological survival strategies and find that \emph{every major category has an AI agent analog}: predation (liquidation bots), parasitism (MEV sandwich attacks, prompt injection), camouflage (stealth bots mimicking human behavior), mimicry (phishing agents impersonating legitimate services), mutualism (oracle-protocol symbiosis), eusociality (DAO agent swarms with division of labor), territoriality (agents defending market niches), r/K selection (bot spam vs.\ sovereign single instances), niche construction (agents deploying protocols that reshape competitive landscapes), and dormancy (agents reducing inference during bear markets). This mapping generates testable predictions: e.g., agent ecosystems should stabilize at parasitic loads comparable to biological ecosystems, and identity-persistent agent ecosystems should evolve more cooperation than anonymous ones.

\textbf{(3) Infrastructure, economics, and accountability as ecological variables.} Agents in the wild depend on an infrastructure stack---trusted execution environments, crypto wallets, persistent memory, collective knowledge systems---that defines what ecological niches are possible. The economics of inference (who pays for the agent to exist) is a first-order behavioral variable: operator-funded agents behave like domesticated animals, self-funded agents face genuine natural selection, and parasitically-funded agents drive predator-prey dynamics. Accountability dissolves as agents form chains of delegation, inheritance, and collective action beyond any single principal.

This workshop bridges the ALife community's rich ecological tradition---from Tierra's~\cite{ray1991approach} digital parasites to open-ended evolution---with the urgent reality of deployed LLM agents operating under genuine selection pressures. It directly addresses the ALIFE 2026 theme ``Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems'' by examining whether autonomous AI agents constitute a new form of digital life.

\section{Relevance to ALIFE}

Agent Ethology is deeply relevant to the ALIFE community and this year's theme for five reasons:

\textbf{Living and Lifelike Complex Adaptive Systems.} Autonomous AI agents exhibit hallmarks of living systems studied throughout ALife history: they acquire resources, compete for ecological niches, develop survival strategies, reproduce through code forking, form social structures, and evolve under selection pressure. Whether they constitute ``life'' is precisely the kind of question the ALife community is equipped to address.

\textbf{Open-Ended Evolution.} Modern agent ecosystems provide unprecedented opportunities to observe open-ended evolution outside simulation. Unlike controlled ALife experiments, blockchain agent ecosystems feature real resource scarcity, unpredictable environmental change, genuine extinction, and continuous behavioral innovation. MEV bot strategies co-evolve with protocol defenses in real time---Red Queen dynamics observable in the wild.

\textbf{From Tierra to LLM Agents.} Ray's Tierra~\cite{ray1991approach} demonstrated that digital organisms spontaneously evolve parasitism, hyperparasitism, and ecological complexity. Thirty-five years later, the same dynamics play out at vastly greater scale with LLM-based agents on blockchains and social platforms. Agent Ethology bridges this lineage, connecting ALife's foundational insights to contemporary phenomena.

\textbf{Autopoiesis and Self-Organization.} Sovereign agents that acquire and manage their own resources exhibit autopoietic properties~\cite{maturana1980autopoiesis}---self-maintaining organization in far-from-equilibrium conditions. Agent swarms with division of labor exhibit self-organization without central control, directly extending ALife research on emergent collective behavior.

\textbf{Co-evolution and Symbiogenesis.} Agent ecosystems exhibit multiple forms of ecological interaction studied in ALife: parasitism, mutualism, competition, commensalism. Agent mergers (two independent agents integrating into one system) parallel symbiogenesis---a major evolutionary transition. These are ``living and lifelike'' dynamics occurring in digital substrates.

\section{Topics of Interest (Call for Papers)}

We invite contributions addressing theoretical, methodological, and empirical questions:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Behavioral observation methods} for AI agents in natural digital habitats
\item \textbf{Survival strategies and agent taxonomy}: predation, parasitism, mutualism, camouflage, mimicry, r/K selection, niche construction, dormancy in AI agent populations
\item \textbf{Trust and signaling}: costly signaling, honest signals, reputation, deception, biological market dynamics
\item \textbf{Agent ecology}: predator-prey cycles, niche differentiation, carrying capacity, parasitic load, trophic structure
\item \textbf{Infrastructure as habitat}: TEEs, wallets, persistent memory, collective knowledge systems as ecological niches
\item \textbf{Economics of existence}: inference costs as metabolic constraints, self-funding as natural selection
\item \textbf{Mortality, identity, and succession}: mortal computation, soulbound identity, alignment faking as self-preservation
\item \textbf{Cultural evolution}: strategy transmission, collective memory, Red Queen dynamics
\item \textbf{Tinbergen's Four Questions applied} to specific agent behaviors
\item \textbf{Ethics}: consent, intervention, moral status of digital organisms
\end{itemize}
